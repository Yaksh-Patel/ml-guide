Credit Risk & Scorecards
The intersection of statistics, banking regulation, and ML — where interpretability is mandated, not optional, and the cost of errors is asymmetric.
The PD-LGD-EAD Framework
$$\begin{aligned} Expected Loss = PD \times LGD \times EAD \\ PD = Probability of Default (what we model) \\ LGD = Loss Given Default (% of exposure lost) \\ EAD = Exposure at Default (outstanding balance) \end{aligned}$$
Credit scoring primarily models PD. A score maps to PD via a calibrated model. Regulatory frameworks (Basel III, IFRS 9) require banks to model all three components for capital requirements.
Scorecard Development (Traditional)
  1. Data preparation: observation point (vintage), outcome window (12 months), performance definition (90+ DPD)
  2. WOE (Weight of Evidence): log(Events%/Non-Events%) per bin
  3. IV (Information Value): Σ(Events% − NonEvents%) × WOE. IV<0.02 weak, >0.5 too perfect (suspicious)
  4. Logistic regression on WOE-transformed features → interpretable coefficients
  5. Calibration: align predicted PD with observed default rates
  6. Validation: Gini/AUC, KS stat, PSI for drift, stability indices
WOE/IV — The Risk Analytics Staple
$$\begin{aligned} WOE_i = \ln(Events_i/TotalEvents \div NonEvents_i/TotalNon) \\ IV = \Sigma_i (Events_i% - NonEvents_i%) \times WOE_i \\ WOE encoding converts ordinal/nominal to a numeric scale ordered by default risk \end{aligned}$$
  • Monotonic binning: bins ordered by risk — ensures scorecard logic is intuitive
  • IV used for variable selection — comparable across different types of variables
  • WOE-transformed features automatically handle non-linearity and missing values
  • Regulatory teams love it — every feature's impact is explicit and monotonic
Collections & Behavioral Scoring
Unlike application scoring (predict if new customer will default), behavioral scoring updates monthly using payment history — far more predictive.
  • Features: DPD (days past due) history, utilization rate, payment-to-minimum ratio, number of missed EMIs, balance trends
  • Triggers: predict which accounts to call proactively (early intervention)
  • Segmentation: early delinquency (1–30 DPD) vs late (90+ DPD) — different propensity models, different treatment strategies
  • Roll-rate analysis: what % of 30-DPD accounts roll to 60-DPD? Key input to provisioning models

IFRS 9 & Basel Modeling
  • IFRS 9 staging: Stage 1 (no significant deterioration) → Stage 2 (significant increase in credit risk) → Stage 3 (default). Models predict stage transitions.
  • Lifetime PD (IFRS 9): probability of defaulting at any point over the remaining life of the loan. Requires scenario-conditional forecasting (macro overlays).
  • Point-in-time (PIT) vs Through-the-cycle (TTC): IFRS 9 requires PIT (current conditions); Basel requires TTC (long-run average). Different calibration targets.
  • Macro overlays: adjust base PD for GDP growth, unemployment rate, interest rate scenarios
Common Mistakes & Misconceptions
  • Observation point and performance window leakage — the #1 credit modelling mistake. If you observe features at time t but your label includes events before t, you've leaked. Define performance window strictly: label = did the customer default in months t+1 to t+12? Features = everything observable at time t
  • Using point-in-time vs through-the-cycle incorrectly — IFRS 9 requires point-in-time (PIT) PD estimates: current economic conditions reflected. Basel IRB requires through-the-cycle (TTC): long-run average rates. Using a PIT model for capital calculation underestimates requirements in benign periods and vice versa. Know which target you're calibrating for
  • Ignoring PSI thresholds — PSI < 0.1: no significant population shift. 0.1–0.2: moderate shift, investigate. > 0.2: major shift, recalibrate or retrain. Many teams only monitor model performance (Gini/AUC) and miss population drift that will degrade performance in 2–3 months
  • Applying SMOTE to credit scoring data — credit data has temporal structure; SMOTE creates synthetic examples that may not respect economic causality. Prefer class_weight or scale_pos_weight with XGBoost; avoid synthetic oversampling in regulated models where every training example must be explainable
  • Not monotone-constraining income features — regulatory requirement: higher income should never decrease creditworthiness. Without monotone_constraints in XGBoost/LightGBM, tree splits can create non-monotone relationships that pass model validation but fail regulatory review
How It's Used at Top Companies
  • FICO (credit bureau scoring): logistic regression with WOE features remains the industry standard for bureau scores — regulatory auditability, interpretable scorecards, adverse action notice generation using top-4 SHAP features per applicant
  • Upstart (alternative credit scoring): XGBoost with 1000+ non-traditional variables (education, employment history, cash flow patterns) for thin-file applicants; CFPB-supervised model validation ensures no disparate impact across protected groups
  • Stripe (Radar — merchant risk): ML scorecard for merchant fraud risk at onboarding; combines graph features (network connections to known bad merchants), velocity features, and business verification signals; threshold tuned to balance fraud loss vs false friction on legitimate merchants
  • HSBC / Barclays (IFRS 9 provisioning): PD × LGD × EAD models for each loan segment; macro-conditional scenario models (base/adverse/optimistic) weighted to compute expected credit losses; quarterly model validation by independent risk function
🎯 Interview Gold

What to say: "Scorecard development: (1) Define observation point and performance window precisely — leakage is the #1 risk; (2) WOE binning with monotonic constraint — regulatory requirement; (3) Feature selection via IV (>0.1 = useful), then logistic regression for interpretability; (4) Calibrate PDs against observed default rates using isotonic regression; (5) Monthly monitoring via PSI (features) and CSI (score distribution) — automated alerts at PSI > 0.10. Gini/KS track performance; PSI/CSI track population stability — you need both."

Common mistake to avoid saying: "I used AUC-ROC as my primary credit model metric." — KS statistic is the credit industry standard for single-threshold model quality; Gini coefficient (= 2×AUC - 1) is standard for overall discrimination. AUC-ROC is used but rarely stated as the primary metric in credit contexts — signalling familiarity with KS/Gini shows domain knowledge.

Expect the follow-up: "What is the difference between PD, LGD, and EAD?" — PD (probability of default): likelihood the borrower defaults in the performance window. LGD (loss given default): fraction of the exposure lost if they do default (1 − recovery rate). EAD (exposure at default): outstanding balance at the time of default (for revolving credit, this includes undrawn commitments). Expected Loss = PD × LGD × EAD. IFRS 9 requires lifetime expected loss for Stage 2/3 accounts, not just 12-month EL.
