Clustering
Unsupervised grouping of data points — find natural structure without labels. Used for customer segmentation, anomaly detection, data exploration, and compression.
K-Means
  1. Initialize k centroids (randomly or K-Means++ for better start)
  2. Assign each point to nearest centroid
  3. Update centroids to mean of assigned points
  4. Repeat until convergence (assignments don't change)
$$\begin{aligned} \text{Minimize: } & \sum_i \sum_{x \in C_i} \|x - \mu_i\|^2 \\ \text{Time: } & O(nkt) \;\text{---}\; n\text{ points, }k\text{ clusters, }t\text{ iterations} \end{aligned}$$
  • Choosing k: Elbow method (inertia vs k), Silhouette score (higher = better defined clusters)
  • Limitations: assumes spherical clusters, sensitive to outliers, must specify k
  • K-Means++: smart initialization that spreads centroids — avoids bad random starts
DBSCAN
Density-Based Spatial Clustering of Applications with Noise.
$$\begin{aligned} \text{Core point:} & \geq \mathit{minPts} \text{ neighbors within } \varepsilon \\ \text{Border point:} & \text{ within } \varepsilon \text{ of a core point} \\ \text{Noise:} & \text{ not within } \varepsilon \text{ of any core point} \end{aligned}$$
  • Discovers arbitrary-shaped clusters
  • Automatically detects outliers (noise points)
  • No need to specify k — just ε and minPts
  • Fails in varying-density datasets; O(n²) naively (use KD-tree for O(n log n))
Hierarchical Clustering
  • Agglomerative (bottom-up): start with each point as cluster, merge closest pairs iteratively
  • Divisive (top-down): start with one cluster, split iteratively
  • Linkage criteria: single (min distance), complete (max), average (mean), Ward (minimize intra-cluster variance)
  • Output: dendrogram — choose number of clusters by cutting at a level
  • No need to specify k upfront; O(n³) — doesn't scale to large data

📖 City Neighborhoods Analogy

K-Means is like drawing equal-radius circles on a city map and calling everything in each circle a "neighborhood" — works if neighborhoods are round. DBSCAN is like a city planner who walks through the streets and groups areas by density — naturally follows the actual urban structure, even if it's irregular.

Common Mistakes & Misconceptions
  • Forgetting to scale features — K-Means and DBSCAN are distance-based; a feature with range [0,10000] dominates one with range [0,1]. Always StandardScaler or MinMaxScaler before clustering
  • Using inertia (elbow) as the sole k selector — inertia always decreases with k. The elbow is often ambiguous. Pair it with silhouette score and domain sense; the "right" k is often a business decision
  • Applying K-Means to non-spherical clusters — K-Means assumes equal-radius, convex clusters. Use DBSCAN or Gaussian Mixture Models for elongated or irregular shapes
  • DBSCAN with bad ε choice — ε too large merges all points into one cluster; too small makes everything noise. Use a k-nearest-neighbor distance plot: sort distances to k-th neighbor, find the "elbow" — that's your ε
  • Evaluating clusters with accuracy — clustering is unsupervised; there are no labels. Use silhouette, Davies-Bouldin, or business-relevant downstream metrics (e.g., churn rate per cluster)
  • Single random init for K-Means — plain random init is sensitive to bad starting positions. Always use K-Means++ (sklearn default) or set n_init=10+ to run multiple inits
How It's Used at Top Companies
  • Spotify: K-Means on audio feature embeddings (tempo, energy, valence) for playlist clustering; centroids become genre or mood "archetypes" used in Discover Weekly recommendations
  • Airbnb: K-Means on host/listing features for market segmentation; clusters inform pricing policy and host support prioritisation by segment
  • Uber: DBSCAN on GPS coordinates to identify ride demand hotspots; noise points (isolated rides) filtered out; density clusters define surge pricing zones
  • Netflix: hierarchical clustering on viewing behaviour to define viewer persona archetypes used in content commissioning decisions
  • Credit card companies: K-Means on spending pattern features for customer lifecycle segmentation; each cluster gets a tailored retention or upsell campaign
🎯 Interview Gold

What to say: "For customer segmentation I default to K-Means with K-Means++ initialization — fast, interpretable, and actionable. I select k by combining the elbow curve, silhouette scores, and business requirements (e.g., 'we have 5 regional sales teams' constrains k). I always StandardScale features first — distance metrics are meaningless on mixed-scale data. For anomaly detection, DBSCAN is superior: noise points are natural anomalies. For unknown cluster shapes, I compare K-Means vs DBSCAN vs Gaussian Mixture Models on silhouette score."

Common mistake to avoid saying: "I chose k=3 because the elbow plot showed 3." — Silhouette + business context must support it. Pure elbow selection signals mechanical thinking.

Expect the follow-up: "How do you validate that your clusters are meaningful?" — Answer: silhouette score for internal validity; downstream business metric (e.g., do clusters have different churn rates?) for external validity; stability check by re-running with different seeds and measuring cluster assignment overlap (Adjusted Rand Index).
