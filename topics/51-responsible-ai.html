Responsible AI & Fairness
ML models embedded in hiring, lending, healthcare, and criminal justice make decisions that affect people's lives. Responsible AI covers the technical, ethical, and governance practices for building systems that are fair, accountable, transparent, and safe. This is increasingly a mandatory competency — regulators, employers, and customers now demand it.
Why Responsible AI Is Not Optional
  • Regulatory pressure: EU AI Act (2024), US Executive Order on AI, NYC Local Law 144 (automated employment decisions) all mandate bias audits and transparency
  • Business risk: ProPublica's COMPAS analysis, Amazon's hiring tool, Apple Card credit discrimination — all caused major reputational damage
  • Technical debt: unfair models are often less accurate on underrepresented groups — fixing fairness frequently improves overall model quality
  • Trust: users and regulators require explainability; a black-box that cannot be audited cannot be deployed in regulated industries

Part 1 — Sources of Bias
Where Bias Enters the ML Pipeline
Data Bias
  • Historical bias: training data reflects past discrimination (e.g. historical hiring data where women were underrepresented in tech)
  • Representation bias: training set doesn't represent the deployment population (e.g. facial recognition trained mostly on light-skinned faces)
  • Measurement bias: proxy features encode protected attributes (e.g. zip code encodes race; arrest records encode policing bias)
  • Label bias: human annotators apply different standards to different groups
Model Bias
  • Aggregation bias: single model trained on heterogeneous groups doesn't work well for any subgroup
  • Evaluation bias: benchmarks don't represent the deployment distribution; good benchmark score ≠ fair in production
  • Algorithmic amplification: small disparities in data get amplified by models optimising for aggregate accuracy
  • Feedback loops: model predictions change future data (e.g. police model predicts crime → more policing → more arrests → model reinforced)
Deployment Bias
  • Population shift: model trained on one population deployed on another
  • Automation bias: humans over-rely on model recommendations even when wrong
  • Use case mismatch: model designed for one purpose repurposed for higher-stakes decisions
  • Missing context: model score used without the uncertainty/confidence information
Part 2 — Fairness Definitions & Metrics
The Impossibility Theorem — You Cannot Satisfy All Fairness Criteria Simultaneously
Chouldechova (2017) and Kleinberg et al. (2016) proved that calibration, equal FPR, and equal FNR cannot all hold simultaneously when base rates differ across groups. Every fairness metric is a tradeoff — choosing one often violates another.
$$\begin{aligned} \text{Let } G \in \{0,1\} &= \text{protected attribute (e.g. sex, race)} \\ \hat{Y} &= \text{model prediction}, \quad Y = \text{true label} \\ \text{If } P(Y=1|G=0) &\neq P(Y=1|G=1) \text{ (different base rates),} \\ \text{then: Calibration} &+ \text{Equal FPR} + \text{Equal FNR cannot all hold} \end{aligned}$$
Practical implication

You must choose which fairness criterion matters most for your use case — there is no universally "fair" model. This is a values and policy decision, not a purely technical one. Document the choice and justify it.

Audit checklist

(1) Identify protected attributes and their proxies. (2) Compute disparate impact ratio — flag <0.8. (3) Compute TPR/FPR gap by subgroup — flag >10%. (4) Check AUC by subgroup — flag gap >3%. (5) Check calibration by subgroup. (6) Inspect SHAP values for protected attribute or proxy features appearing as top predictors.

Part 4 — Bias Mitigation Techniques
Three Stages of Mitigation
Pre-processing (Data)
  • Reweighting: assign higher sample weights to underrepresented groups so model treats them equally
  • Resampling: oversample minority group, undersample majority to balance training distribution
  • Data augmentation: generate synthetic examples for underrepresented subgroups
  • Disparate impact remover: transform features to remove correlation with protected attribute while preserving rank ordering (AIF360)
  • Remove proxy features: drop features that are highly correlated with protected attribute (zip code, school name)
In-processing (Model)
  • Adversarial debiasing: train predictor + adversary; adversary tries to predict protected attribute from predictions; predictor trained to fool adversary
  • Constrained optimisation: add fairness constraint to loss function; optimise accuracy subject to demographic parity ≤ ε (Fairlearn ExponentiatedGradient)
  • Meta-fair classifier: optimise directly for a fairness metric instead of accuracy
  • Prejudice remover: add fairness-aware regularisation term to penalise dependence on protected attribute
Post-processing (Output)
  • Threshold optimisation: set different classification thresholds per group to equalise TPR or FPR (Fairlearn ThresholdOptimizer)
  • Reject option classification: near-threshold predictions go to a human reviewer rather than being decided by the model
  • Calibration per group: apply Platt scaling separately for each subgroup so scores are calibrated within each group
  • Output re-ranking: re-rank model outputs to ensure diversity across protected attributes (search/recommendation)
Which mitigation to choose

Post-processing (threshold optimisation) is the easiest to apply without retraining — start here. Pre-processing (reweighting) is the most model-agnostic. In-processing gives the best accuracy-fairness tradeoff but requires modifying training. Always measure the accuracy cost of mitigation — there is always a tradeoff.

Part 6 — Model Cards, Governance & Regulatory Landscape
Model Cards — Standardised Transparency Documentation
Proposed by Mitchell et al. (Google, 2019). A model card is a short document accompanying a model that communicates intended use, performance characteristics by subgroup, and known limitations.
Required sections
  • Model details: architecture, training data, parameters, version
  • Intended use: primary use cases, out-of-scope uses
  • Performance metrics: accuracy, AUC, F1 — overall AND per subgroup
  • Evaluation data: what dataset was used for evaluation; how representative is it
  • Ethical considerations: known biases, fairness analysis results, mitigation applied
  • Limitations: what populations or scenarios the model works poorly on
  • Caveats & recommendations: how the model should and shouldn't be used
Who requires model cards
  • HuggingFace: model cards on Model Hub (best practice, increasingly expected)
  • Google Cloud AI: required for models deployed via Vertex AI Model Registry
  • EU AI Act: technical documentation requirement for high-risk AI systems
  • US NIST AI RMF: recommends model cards as part of AI Risk Management Framework
  • Financial regulators (OCC, CFPB): model risk management guidance requires documentation of model limitations and validation

Common Mistakes & Misconceptions
  • "We didn't include protected attributes so the model is fair" — proxy variables (zip code, name, browsing history) encode protected attributes. Omitting the feature does not remove the bias
  • "High overall accuracy = fair" — a model can have 95% accuracy overall while performing at 70% for a minority group. Always disaggregate metrics by subgroup
  • "Fairness and accuracy are always in tension" — often untrue. Bias frequently causes worse performance on underrepresented groups. Fixing data representation often improves both
  • "One fairness metric is enough" — each metric encodes a different moral intuition. Report multiple; justify which matters most for your use case
  • "Post-deployment, fairness is someone else's problem" — distribution shift can introduce new biases over time. Monitor fairness metrics in production alongside accuracy metrics
  • "Explainability = fairness" — SHAP can explain a biased model clearly. Transparency and fairness are related but distinct properties

🎯 Interview Gold

What is algorithmic fairness? "Algorithmic fairness is the study of whether ML model decisions are equitable across demographic groups. There are multiple mathematical definitions — demographic parity (equal positive rates), equal opportunity (equal TPR), equalised odds (equal TPR and FPR), and calibration (probabilities match outcomes per group). Critically, these definitions are mutually exclusive when base rates differ — a fundamental theorem in fairness — so choosing which metric to optimise is a values and policy decision, not a technical one."

How do you audit a model for bias? "I'd (1) identify protected attributes and their proxies — zip code encoding race, names encoding gender. (2) Compute disparate impact ratio — US EEOC flags anything below 0.8. (3) Compute TPR and FPR gaps by subgroup using Fairlearn's MetricFrame. (4) Check AUC and calibration per group. (5) Inspect SHAP values for protected attribute proxies appearing as top features. Then mitigate — usually starting with threshold optimisation (easiest, no retraining) or sample reweighting (next simplest)."

Fairness-accuracy tradeoff? "Often overstated. In many cases, bias in training data causes the model to perform worse on underrepresented groups — fixing bias via reweighting or better data collection improves both fairness AND accuracy for those groups. The tradeoff is real but smaller than assumed, and the framing 'accuracy or fairness' is frequently a false dilemma."

Common follow-up — 'What's disparate impact?' "Disparate impact is when a facially neutral policy disproportionately disadvantages a protected group, regardless of intent. In ML: the disparate impact ratio is the positive prediction rate for the minority group divided by the rate for the majority group. Below 0.8 triggers the US 4/5ths rule — presumptive evidence of illegal discrimination under Title VII and ECOA."
