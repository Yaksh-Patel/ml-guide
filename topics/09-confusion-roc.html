AUC-ROC
Plots TPR vs FPR at every threshold. Area = P(positive scores higher than random negative).
  • AUC=1: perfect | AUC=0.5: random | AUC<0.5: flip predictions
  • Weakness: inflated by large TN pool in imbalanced data
  • Use when: balanced classes, overall ranking quality
PR-AUC (Precision-Recall)
Plots Precision vs Recall. Neither formula touches TN — immune to class imbalance effect.
  • Baseline = prevalence of positives (e.g. 1% for fraud)
  • Use when: imbalanced — fraud, disease, spam detection
  • Higher is better; compare to random baseline, not to 0.5
KS Statistic — Banking's Favorite
Maximum separation between CDF of positive scores and CDF of negative scores. Identifies the score threshold that best separates the classes.
  • Range: 0–100. KS = 0: no separation. KS = 100: perfect.
  • Used in credit scoring to find the optimal approval cutoff score
  • Weak point: only captures max at one threshold; ignores rest of the curve
📖 Rice Pile Analogy

KS finds the score where the red pile (defaulters) and blue pile (good customers) diverge most. A lender picks exactly that score as the loan approval cutoff.

Common Mistakes & Misconceptions
  • Reporting AUC-ROC without context on class balance — AUC-ROC of 0.95 on a 1% positive rate dataset tells you very little about minority class recall. Always pair with PR-AUC and report the positive rate
  • Treating 0.5 as the classification threshold — sklearn's default threshold of 0.5 is almost never optimal. Tune the threshold on a validation set to maximize your business metric (e.g., precision at 80% recall); never ship with the default
  • Confusing precision and recall direction — precision = of what we predicted positive, how many were correct? Recall = of all actual positives, how many did we catch? Common interview confusion: "we want to minimize false negatives" = maximize recall, not precision
  • Using micro-averaged F1 on imbalanced multiclass — micro-average is dominated by the large majority class. Use macro-average (equal weight per class) or weighted-average (weight by support) depending on whether rare classes matter equally
  • Comparing AUC-ROC across very different datasets — AUC-ROC is dataset-specific. A 0.85 model on a hard problem may outperform a 0.95 model on an easy one. Always compare relative to a sensible baseline on the same data
How It's Used at Top Companies
  • PayPal / Stripe (fraud): operate at a fixed recall threshold (catch ≥90% of fraud) and maximise precision within that constraint — the confusion matrix is monitored daily; threshold is adjusted monthly as fraud patterns drift
  • Google (medical AI): dermatology cancer detection reported at both AUC-ROC and sensitivity@specificity=90% — the fixed-operating-point metric is more clinically meaningful than aggregate AUC
  • Amazon (content moderation): PR curve used to select operating point: high-precision mode for auto-removal, lower-precision mode for flagging for human review — two thresholds, one model
  • Credit bureaus (FICO, Experian): KS statistic is the regulatory standard for scorecard quality; models must achieve KS ≥ 30 to be approved for production lending decisions
🎯 Interview Gold

What to say: "With 99% negatives, AUC-ROC can be 0.97 for a mediocre model — the TN pool inflates specificity. PR-AUC is more honest: it ignores true negatives entirely. For credit risk I report three metrics: AUC-ROC for overall ranking quality, KS for the single best threshold, and PR-AUC for honest minority-class performance. I never use the default 0.5 threshold — I tune it on a validation set to match the business operating point (e.g., recall=90% then maximise precision)."

Common mistake to avoid saying: "I optimized F1 score." — F1 weights precision and recall equally, which implies equal cost to false positives and false negatives. That's almost never the business reality. Say instead: "I chose the threshold that maximized precision at our minimum acceptable recall of X%."

Expect the follow-up: "Walk me through the ROC curve construction." — Answer: for each possible threshold from 1→0, compute TPR (recall = TP/P) and FPR (= FP/N). Plot TPR vs FPR. The area under this curve is AUC-ROC. Random classifier = diagonal line (AUC=0.5). Perfect classifier = top-left corner (AUC=1.0). The curve shows the tradeoff between catching positives and tolerating false alarms across all thresholds.
