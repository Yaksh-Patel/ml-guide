$$\begin{aligned} \text{TPR/Recall} &= TP/(TP+FN) \quad \leftarrow \text{sensitivity} \\ \text{FPR} &= FP/(FP+TN) \quad \leftarrow 1-\text{specificity} \\ \text{Specificity} &= TN/(TN+FP) \\ \text{Precision} &= TP/(TP+FP) \\ F_1 &= 2 \cdot TP \;/\; (2 \cdot TP + FP + FN) \end{aligned}$$
AUC-ROC
Plots TPR vs FPR at every threshold. Area = P(positive scores higher than random negative).
  • AUC=1: perfect | AUC=0.5: random | AUC<0.5: flip predictions
  • Weakness: inflated by large TN pool in imbalanced data
  • Use when: balanced classes, overall ranking quality
PR-AUC (Precision-Recall)
Plots Precision vs Recall. Neither formula touches TN — immune to class imbalance effect.
  • Baseline = prevalence of positives (e.g. 1% for fraud)
  • Use when: imbalanced — fraud, disease, spam detection
  • Higher is better; compare to random baseline, not to 0.5
KS Statistic — Banking's Favorite
Maximum separation between CDF of positive scores and CDF of negative scores. Identifies the score threshold that best separates the classes.
  • Range: 0–100. KS = 0: no separation. KS = 100: perfect.
  • Used in credit scoring to find the optimal approval cutoff score
  • Weak point: only captures max at one threshold; ignores rest of the curve
📖 Rice Pile Analogy

KS finds the score where the red pile (defaulters) and blue pile (good customers) diverge most. A lender picks exactly that score as the loan approval cutoff.

🎯 Interview Gold

"With 99% negatives and 1% positives (fraud), AUC-ROC can be 0.97 for a mediocre model because all the easy TN predictions inflate it. PR-AUC will be much lower and more honest. For credit risk I report all three: AUC-ROC for model ranking quality, KS for the optimal decision threshold, and PR-AUC for a fair view of how well we actually find the rare defaulters."
