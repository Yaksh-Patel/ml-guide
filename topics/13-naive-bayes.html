Naive Bayes Classifier
Applies Bayes' theorem with a "naive" independence assumption — and works shockingly well despite that assumption being almost always wrong.
The Algorithm
$$\begin{aligned} P(y \mid x_1,\ldots,x_n) &\propto P(y) \times \prod_i P(x_i \mid y) \\ \hat{y} &= \arg\max_y \bigl[\log P(y) + \textstyle\sum_i \log P(x_i \mid y)\bigr] \\ &\quad\text{(use log-sum to avoid numerical underflow)} \end{aligned}$$
The "naive" assumption: features x₁,...,xₙ are conditionally independent given y. This is almost never true, yet the classifier often dominates.

Limitations
  • Zero-frequency problem: if a feature-class combo never appeared in training, P = 0 kills the whole product → fix with Laplace smoothing (+1 to all counts)
  • Poor probability calibration — outputs aren't well-calibrated
  • Independence assumption breaks with correlated features
  • Cannot capture feature interactions
📖 Spam Filter Analogy

You see "FREE MONEY NIGERIA" in an email. NB asks: what % of spam had "FREE"? "MONEY"? "NIGERIA"? Multiply these probabilities (naively assuming word independence). Compare to legit email. Works because the words signal spam strongly — independence doesn't matter when signals are loud.

Common Mistakes & Misconceptions
  • Forgetting Laplace smoothing — if any word appears in test but never in training for a class, P=0 zeroes the entire posterior. Always add-1 (Laplace) or add-α (Lidstone) smoothing to all counts
  • Not using log-probabilities — multiplying hundreds of small probabilities causes floating-point underflow to zero. Always work in log-space: sum log-probabilities, not multiply raw probabilities
  • "Naive" means it's a bad model — despite the violated independence assumption, Naive Bayes is competitive or best-in-class for text classification, especially with small training sets. It's "naive" about features, not naive as a model
  • Using Gaussian NB on bag-of-words features — word count features are discrete and non-negative; Multinomial NB is the correct variant. Gaussian NB assumes continuous normally-distributed features
  • Ignoring poor probability calibration — NB probabilities are often extreme (near 0 or 1) because of the independence assumption compounding. Never use NB raw probabilities as calibrated outputs; apply Platt scaling if probabilities matter
How It's Used at Top Companies
  • Email spam filtering (Gmail, Outlook): Naive Bayes was the original spam filter backbone through the 2000s–2010s; still used as a lightweight real-time filter layer before deeper neural classifiers
  • Medical diagnosis (low-data settings): when labeled patient data is scarce, Naive Bayes outperforms complex models; used in clinical text classification for ICD code prediction from short notes
  • News / content categorisation: real-time topic tagging at media companies (Reuters, Bloomberg) using Multinomial NB on TF-IDF vectors — microsecond inference, no GPU needed
  • Sentiment analysis baselines: Naive Bayes with bigrams is a strong baseline for review sentiment; frequently cited in academic papers as the comparator for new neural approaches
🎯 Interview Gold

What to say: "Naive Bayes is my first-try classifier for text with small training sets. The independence assumption is almost always wrong, but it rarely matters in practice — strong individual feature signals dominate. Two implementation must-dos: Laplace smoothing (prevent zero probabilities) and log-space computation (prevent underflow). For text, Multinomial NB on TF or count features; for continuous features, Gaussian NB. It trains in O(n·d) and predicts in O(d) — unbeatable for real-time low-latency applications."

Common mistake to avoid saying: "Naive Bayes is too simple for serious use." — It's one of the few models that works well with very small labeled datasets, and is still production-relevant for real-time text classification where latency matters more than 1–2% accuracy.

Expect the follow-up: "Why does Naive Bayes work well despite the independence assumption being wrong?" — Answer: even when feature probabilities are wrong in absolute terms, the ranking of P(class|features) across classes is often correct. Classification only needs the right class to win the argmax, not accurate individual probabilities. The model is miscalibrated but correctly ordered.
