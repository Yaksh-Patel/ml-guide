Probability Theory
The mathematical language of uncertainty — every ML model is fundamentally making probabilistic statements, whether it admits it or not.
Basic Rules
$$\begin{aligned} \mathbb{P}(A \cup B) = \mathbb{P}(A) + \mathbb{P}(B) - \mathbb{P}(A \cap B) \\ \mathbb{P}(A \cap B) = \mathbb{P}(A) \cdot \mathbb{P}(B \mid A) \\ \mathbb{P}(A \mid B) = \mathbb{P}(A \cap B) / \mathbb{P}(B) \\ \text{Independent: } \mathbb{P}(A \cap B) = \mathbb{P}(A) \cdot \mathbb{P}(B) \end{aligned}$$
Bayes' Theorem
$$\begin{aligned} P(H \mid E) = \frac{P(E \mid H) \cdot P(H)}{P(E)} \\[6pt] \text{Posterior} = \frac{\text{Likelihood} \times \text{Prior}}{\text{Evidence}} \end{aligned}$$
📖 Rare Disease Trap

A test is 99% accurate for a disease affecting 1% of people. You test positive. P(sick|positive) = 0.99×0.01/(0.99×0.01+0.01×0.99) ≈ 50%! The low prior (1%) kills the intuition. Bayes corrects your panic.

Expectation & Variance
$$\begin{aligned} E[X] = \sum x_i P(x_i) \quad \text{(discrete)} \\ \mathrm{Var}(X) = E[X^2] - (E[X])^2 \\ \mathrm{Var}(aX+b) = a^2 \mathrm{Var}(X) \\ \mathrm{Cov}(X,Y) = E\!\left[(X - \mu_x)(Y - \mu_y)\right] \\ \rho(X,Y) = \mathrm{Cov}(X,Y) / (\sigma_x \sigma_y) \quad \text{(Pearson r)} \end{aligned}$$
MLE vs MAP
  • MLE: θ̂ = argmax P(data|θ). No prior. Logistic regression minimizes CE = maximizes Bernoulli log-likelihood.
  • MAP: θ̂ = argmax P(θ|data). Includes prior.
💡 Key insight

Ridge regression (L2) = MAP with Gaussian prior. Lasso (L1) = MAP with Laplace prior. Math and ML are the same thing.

Common Mistakes & Misconceptions
  • Confusing P(A|B) and P(B|A) — the base rate fallacy. P(disease|positive test) ≠ P(positive test|disease). Bayes' theorem is needed to convert sensitivity/specificity into the probability that matters clinically. A test with 99% sensitivity still has a low positive predictive value if the disease prevalence is 0.1%
  • Independence vs uncorrelated — if X and Y are independent, they are uncorrelated. But uncorrelated does NOT imply independent (only for jointly Gaussian). Two variables can have zero covariance but strong non-linear dependence
  • Treating KL divergence as a symmetric distance — KL(P||Q) ≠ KL(Q||P) in general; KL divergence is not a metric. In variational inference, the direction matters: KL(q||p) (forward) vs KL(p||q) (reverse) produce different approximation behaviours (mean-seeking vs mode-seeking)
  • The law of large numbers guarantees accuracy on small samples — LLN only applies asymptotically (n → ∞). For small samples, sample statistics have high variance. Confidence intervals quantify this uncertainty; always report them alongside point estimates
  • Assuming Gaussian distributions everywhere — many real-world quantities are heavy-tailed (financial returns, word frequencies, network degrees). Modelling them as Gaussian leads to dramatically underestimated tail risk. Check kurtosis and use appropriate distributions (log-normal, Pareto, Student-t)
How It's Used at Top Companies
  • Google (Bayesian optimisation): Gaussian Process prior over the objective function; posterior updated with each observation; acquisition function (EI, UCB) derived from the posterior distribution — probability theory is the mathematical foundation of Bayesian hyperparameter optimisation
  • Netflix (content modelling): topic models (LDA — Latent Dirichlet Allocation) use Dirichlet priors over document-topic distributions; Bayesian generative model for content tagging and recommendation diversity
  • OpenAI (VAEs / diffusion models): both use KL divergence as a core component — VAE ELBO = reconstruction loss + KL(q(z|x) || p(z)); diffusion model training = matching a learned denoising distribution to a Gaussian noise process
  • Finance (risk management): Value at Risk (VaR) and Expected Shortfall are probability-theoretic measures; heavy-tailed Student-t distributions used for financial return modelling after Gaussian VaR dramatically underestimated tail risk in 2008
🎯 Interview Gold

What to say: "Cross-entropy loss comes from maximising log-likelihood under a Bernoulli/Categorical distribution — minimising cross-entropy IS maximum likelihood estimation. KL divergence measures distribution divergence — used in VAEs (ELBO regularisation), knowledge distillation (matching teacher logits), and RL (PPO's KL penalty that prevents policy updates from straying too far). When I see a new loss function, my first question is: what probability distribution does this implicitly assume about the data?"

Common mistake to avoid saying: "KL divergence is symmetric." — KL(P||Q) ≠ KL(Q||P). The direction matters: forward KL (used in VI) is mean-seeking (fits a distribution covering all modes); reverse KL is mode-seeking (fits the highest-probability mode only). In practice, RLHF's KL penalty uses KL(π_new || π_ref) to keep the fine-tuned policy close to the reference policy.

Expect the follow-up: "What's the difference between MLE and MAP estimation?" — Answer: MLE maximises P(data|θ) — no prior on θ, purely data-driven. MAP maximises P(θ|data) ∝ P(data|θ)·P(θ) — includes a prior on θ. For a Gaussian prior on weights, MAP is equivalent to L2 (Ridge) regularisation. For a Laplace prior, MAP is equivalent to L1 (Lasso). Regularisation is Bayesian reasoning in disguise.
