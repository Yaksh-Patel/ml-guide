Optimization Algorithms
How parameters update — the choice of optimizer affects convergence speed, final performance, and training stability. Learning rate is the single most important hyperparameter.
SGD — Stochastic Gradient Descent
$$\begin{aligned} \theta \leftarrow \theta - \alpha\nabla L(\theta; x_{\text{batch}}) \quad \text{[vanilla]} \\ v \leftarrow \beta v - \alpha\nabla L(\theta; x_{\text{batch}}) \quad \text{[momentum]} \\ \theta \leftarrow \theta + v \end{aligned}$$
Vanilla SGD: update with gradient from mini-batch. Noisy but regularizing. Momentum: builds speed in consistent directions, dampens oscillations. β=0.9 is standard.
RMSprop
$$\begin{aligned} s \leftarrow \beta s + (1-\beta)\nabla L^2 \\ \theta \leftarrow \theta - \alpha\nabla L / (\sqrt{s + \varepsilon)} \end{aligned}$$
Divides learning rate by running RMS of past gradients. Adapts learning rate per parameter. Good for RNNs — non-stationary objectives with noisy gradients.
Adam (Adaptive Moment Estimation)
$$\begin{aligned} m \leftarrow \beta_1 m + (1-\beta_1)\nabla L \quad \text{[1st moment / mean]} \\ v \leftarrow \beta_2 v + (1-\beta_2)(\nabla L)^2 \quad \text{[2nd moment / variance]} \\ \hat{m} = m/(1-\beta_1^t) \\ \hat{v} = v/(1-\beta_2^t) \\ \theta \leftarrow \theta - \alpha \cdot \hat{m}/(\sqrt{\hat{v} + \varepsilon} \\ Defaults: \beta_1=0.9, \beta_2=0.999, \varepsilon=1e-8 \end{aligned}$$

📖 Mountain Descent Analogy

SGD: walking downhill blindly — fast but zigzags unpredictably. Momentum: rolling a ball — builds speed on gentle slopes. Adam: smart GPS-equipped hiker — adapts step size to terrain, big steps on flat ground, tiny steps on steep cliffs. Usually gets to the valley fastest — but SGD with a good schedule sometimes finds a better valley.

Common Mistakes & Misconceptions
  • Using Adam's built-in weight decay for regularization — Adam with L2 (weight_decay in PyTorch) does not apply weight decay correctly: the adaptive learning rates interact with the penalty, causing the effective decay to vary across parameters. Use AdamW (decoupled weight decay) for correct L2 regularization with Adam
  • Not using learning rate warmup for transformers — starting with the full learning rate causes huge gradient updates at initialisation (attention weights are near-uniform, gradients are large). Always warm up for 1–5% of total training steps, then decay. SGD-based CNN training doesn't need warmup
  • "Adam always converges faster than SGD" — true early in training, but Adam often converges to flatter (and sometimes worse) minima than SGD with momentum + careful schedule. For ResNets/image tasks, well-tuned SGD + cosine annealing often beats Adam on final test accuracy
  • Forgetting to tune the learning rate for Adam — Adam's default lr=1e-3 is a reasonable starting point but is not universally optimal. The learning rate is still the most important hyperparameter even with adaptive optimizers. Always tune it
  • Using the same learning rate for all parameter groups — in fine-tuning, lower learning rates for pretrained layers and higher rates for new layers is standard practice (differential learning rates). Using a single global rate either under-trains new layers or destroys pretrained representations
How It's Used at Top Companies
  • OpenAI / Anthropic / Google (LLM training): AdamW with β₁=0.9, β₂=0.95 (not the default 0.999), cosine decay with linear warmup — this specific recipe (from GPT-3 paper) is now standard across all large language model training runs; β₂=0.95 gives faster adaptation to gradient changes in long training runs
  • Meta (PyTorch — FSDP training): gradient clipping (max_norm=1.0) applied before every AdamW step across all large-scale distributed training; prevents rare but catastrophic gradient spikes that can corrupt model state
  • Google (vision — ViT, EfficientNet): SGD + momentum + cosine annealing for CNNs; AdamW for Vision Transformers — the optimizer choice is architecture-dependent, not a universal constant
  • Hugging Face (fine-tuning LLMs): differential learning rates standard in Trainer API — top transformer layers get the base lr; bottom layers get lr × 0.1; new task-specific head gets lr × 10 — prevents catastrophic forgetting of pretrained representations
🎯 Interview Gold

What to say: "AdamW is my default for transformer training — decoupled weight decay is critical (Adam's built-in L2 doesn't apply correctly due to adaptive rate interaction). For ResNets and image classification, SGD + cosine annealing often achieves better final accuracy than Adam — Adam converges faster but to a slightly worse minimum. Learning rate is always the most important hyperparameter even with adaptive optimizers. For transformers: always warm up (1–5% of steps) to avoid catastrophic initialisation updates, then cosine decay."

Common mistake to avoid saying: "I used Adam with weight_decay for L2 regularization." — That's the original Adam's L2, which doesn't work correctly. AdamW decouples the weight decay from the gradient update, applying it correctly. These are meaningfully different especially at large scale.

Expect the follow-up: "Why does Adam sometimes generalise worse than SGD?" — Answer: Adam adapts learning rates per parameter based on gradient history, which can cause it to over-fit to the gradient statistics of the training set. SGD with momentum finds sharper but more consistent minima; with the right schedule, these can generalise better. The "sharpness" of minima correlates with generalisation — Adam tends to find flatter but potentially worse generalising solutions on vision tasks.
