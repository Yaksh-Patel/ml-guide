Time Series Modeling
Forecasting and anomaly detection where temporal ordering matters — random splits destroy the structure, and stationarity is the prerequisite most beginners skip.
Stationarity — The Prerequisite
A time series is stationary if its mean, variance, and autocorrelation structure are constant over time. Most raw series are non-stationary (trends, seasonality).
$$\begin{aligned} \text{Test: Augmented Dickey-Fuller (ADF) test} \\ H_0\text{: series has unit root (non-stationary)} \\ p < 0.05\text{: reject }H_0 \rightarrow \text{series is stationary} \\ \text{Transformations: differencing, }\log\text{, seasonal decomposition (STL)} \end{aligned}$$
ARIMA (Statistical)
$$\begin{aligned} \text{ARIMA}(p,d,q)\text{:} \\ p = \text{autoregressive terms (AR): lag } p \text{ correlations} \\ d = \text{differencing order (to achieve stationarity)} \\ q = \text{moving average terms (MA): lag } q \text{ error correlations} \\ \text{SARIMA}(p,d,q)(P,D,Q)_m\text{: seasonal variant} \end{aligned}$$
  • Identify p, d, q from ACF/PACF plots
  • or auto.arima() / pmdarima for automatic selection
  • Best for: univariate, short-term, linear relationships
  • Weakness: struggles with non-linear patterns, multiple seasonalities
Prophet (Facebook/Meta)
$$\begin{aligned} y(t) = \text{trend}(t) + \text{seasonality}(t) + \text{holidays}(t) + \text{noise} \\ \text{trend} = \text{piecewise linear or logistic growth} \\ \text{seasonality} = \text{Fourier series decomposition} \end{aligned}$$
  • Handles daily, weekly, yearly seasonality automatically
  • Robust to missing data and outliers
  • Easy to add custom holidays and changepoints
  • Interpretable decomposition — great for business users
  • Best for: business metrics, web traffic, sales forecasting

Time Series Evaluation — Critical Rules
  • Never random split: always use temporal splits — train before validation date, validate before test date
  • Walk-forward validation: retrain on expanding window, validate on next period
  • Metrics: MAE (interpretable), MAPE (if no near-zero values), sMAPE (symmetric), RMSE (penalizes peaks)
  • Leakage danger: future statistics (mean over full series, etc.) in features
📖 EMI Collection Analogy

Predicting which customers will miss their next EMI: use lag features (days since last payment, rolling 3-month payment rate), seasonal patterns (salary cycle — payments spike on 1st), macroeconomic indicators. ARIMA fails here because it's univariate. XGBoost on lag features + Prophet seasonal decomposition wins.

Common Mistakes & Misconceptions
  • Using random train/test split on time series — the #1 time series mistake. Random splitting leaks future data into training, producing absurdly optimistic metrics. Always use a strict temporal split: train on past, validate on a future period, test on the most recent period
  • Creating lag features that use future values — subtle leakage. If you compute a 7-day rolling mean at time t using days t-3 to t+3, you've leaked future values. Always use exclusively past data: rolling_mean(t-7 to t-1), never centred windows in training features
  • "ARIMA is obsolete" — ARIMA remains a strong baseline for short-horizon univariate forecasting and is interpretable. For multivariate or long-horizon problems, ML methods win. Always benchmark against ARIMA and seasonal naive (last year same period) — they are surprisingly hard to beat
  • Not testing for stationarity before ARIMA — ARIMA assumes a stationary series. Non-stationary data (trending, changing variance) needs differencing (ARIMA's 'd' parameter) or log transformation first. Always run an ADF test; reject stationarity before differencing
  • Ignoring data collection artifacts — weekends, public holidays, reporting delays, and fiscal quarters cause artificial patterns that a model shouldn't learn as signal. Always visualise the series for outliers and calendar effects before modelling
How It's Used at Top Companies
  • Uber (demand forecasting): TFT (Temporal Fusion Transformer) for city-level ride demand — combines LSTM-based temporal processing with attention over static metadata (city type, day-of-week) and multi-horizon outputs; deployed globally for dynamic pricing and driver positioning
  • Amazon (inventory management): DeepAR (autoregressive RNN) trained jointly over thousands of related time series; learns cross-series patterns (correlated demand across product categories) that univariate ARIMA cannot capture
  • Meta (Prophet): open-sourced their internal Prophet model — additive decomposition (trend + seasonality + holidays) with automatic changepoint detection; non-technical teams can use it without time series expertise; widely used for business KPI forecasting
  • Credit risk / banking: ARIMA + macro overlays for IFRS 9 lifetime PD forecasting; regulatory requirement that models be interpretable and auditable prevents use of black-box ML for regulatory capital calculations
🎯 Interview Gold

What to say: "My time series stack: (1) EDA with STL decomposition — understand trend, seasonality, residuals; (2) ADF test for stationarity; (3) ARIMA / seasonal naive as hard-to-beat baselines; (4) XGBoost on lag features (lag 1, 7, 28 days + rolling means) as a strong ML baseline; (5) TFT or N-HiTS for complex multivariate / long-horizon. Walk-forward validation is non-negotiable — random splits produce fantasy metrics. Always check for calendar artifacts (holidays, weekends, reporting delays) in exploratory analysis."

Common mistake to avoid saying: "I split the time series randomly 80/20." — This is temporal leakage. Future data trains the model; validation metrics are meaningless. Always: train = past, validation = future, test = most recent period.

Expect the follow-up: "How do you create features for a time series ML model?" — Answer: lag features (y at t-1, t-7, t-28), rolling statistics (mean/std over past 7/30/90 days), calendar features (hour, day-of-week, month, is_holiday), and target-encoded categorical context (store_id, product_category). All features must use only past data relative to the prediction point — no future leakage. Lag features implicitly encode autocorrelation that ARIMA models explicitly; ML models discover this from data.
