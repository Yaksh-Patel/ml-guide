Decision Trees
Recursive binary partitioning of feature space — the building block of all tree-based ensemble methods, and the only non-linear model that's truly self-explanatory.
Split Criteria
Gini Impurity
$$\begin{aligned} Gini = 1 - \sum_i p_i^2 \\ \text{Range: } [0,\; 0.5]. \text{ Lower = purer.} \\ Gini = 0 \rightarrow \text{leaf has only one class} \end{aligned}$$
Entropy / Information Gain
$$\begin{aligned} H = -\sum_i p_i \log_2(p_i) \\ IG = H(\text{parent}) - \sum_i w_i \cdot H(\text{child}_i) \\ \text{Choose split that maximizes } IG \end{aligned}$$
Gini vs Entropy: Gini is computationally cheaper; Entropy is more principled (information-theoretic). In practice, results are nearly identical. sklearn uses Gini by default.
Strengths
  • Fully interpretable — literally draw the tree
  • No feature scaling needed (rank-based splits)
  • Handles mixed data types natively
  • Captures non-linear relationships and interactions
  • No distributional assumptions
  • Handles missing values (with impurity trick)
Weaknesses
  • High variance — small data change = completely different tree
  • Overfits without pruning (grows until leaves are pure)
  • Cannot extrapolate beyond training range
  • Biased toward high-cardinality features in split selection
  • Piecewise constant predictions — stepped, not smooth

📖 20 Questions Analogy

A decision tree plays 20 Questions: "Is income > 50K?" → "Is credit score > 700?" → "Has ever defaulted?" Each question splits the universe into purer subsets. Best questions are the ones that most cleanly separate the classes — those are the high information gain splits.

Common Mistakes & Misconceptions
  • Using a single deep tree in production — a fully grown tree memorizes training data and generalizes poorly. Single trees are only used when strict interpretability is required (depth ≤ 3); otherwise, use an ensemble
  • Gini vs Entropy debate — in practice, they almost always produce the same trees. Do not waste interview time defending one over the other; mention sklearn uses Gini by default and results are empirically equivalent
  • Not pruning before deploying an interpretable tree — an unpruned depth-15 tree is not interpretable. Depth 3–4 gives <16 leaves — a human can actually read it. Use ccp_alpha with CV to find the optimal pruned size
  • Assuming feature importance from a single tree is reliable — a single tree's feature importance is noisy (different random splits → different importances). Aggregate over a Random Forest for stability
  • Decision trees for regression extrapolation — trees predict the mean of training leaf members; they cannot predict beyond the training range. For extrapolation, linear models are required
How It's Used at Top Companies
  • Banking / credit (Basel III compliance): depth-3 or depth-4 decision trees as the required interpretable component of credit decision systems; regulators demand the ability to explain any individual decision
  • Healthcare diagnostics: decision trees for clinical decision support — the tree structure maps directly to a clinical protocol that nurses and doctors can follow without a computer
  • XGBoost / LightGBM internals: every boosted model is a collection of shallow decision trees (depth 3–6); understanding tree splitting is prerequisite to understanding any tree-based ensemble
  • A/B test segmentation: decision trees used to find subgroups where a treatment effect is strongest (heterogeneous treatment effects) — called "uplift trees" in growth and marketing
🎯 Interview Gold

What to say: "Decision trees are the most interpretable non-linear model — I can show a risk committee the literal decision logic. But a single unconstrained tree has unacceptably high variance (small data perturbations change the entire structure). My rule: depth-3 trees for regulatory interpretability; deeper trees only as base learners inside an ensemble (Random Forest, XGBoost) where averaging/boosting controls the variance. Split criterion is usually Gini — empirically equivalent to entropy but faster to compute."

Common mistake to avoid saying: "I'll grow the tree until leaves are pure, then prune." — This is expensive and often leads to over-pruning. Prefer setting max_depth and min_samples_leaf directly, or use cost-complexity pruning (ccp_alpha) tuned via CV.

Expect the follow-up: "Why does a decision tree have high variance?" — Answer: greedy recursive splitting is unstable — a slightly different training set can produce a completely different top split, leading to an entirely different tree. Ensembles (bagging, boosting) address this by averaging over many such trees.
