Evaluation Metrics
Choosing the wrong metric is one of the most common mistakes in applied ML. The metric must reflect business reality, not just mathematical convenience.
Regression Metrics
$$\begin{aligned} \text{MAE} &= \frac{1}{n}\sum|y_i - \hat{y}_i| \\ \text{MSE} &= \frac{1}{n}\sum(y_i - \hat{y}_i)^2 \\ \text{RMSE} &= \sqrt{\text{MSE}} \\ R^2 &= 1 - \frac{SS_\text{res}}{SS_\text{tot}} \quad [0 = \text{mean model},\; 1 = \text{perfect}] \\ \text{MAPE} &= \frac{1}{n}\sum\frac{|y_i - \hat{y}_i|}{|y_i|} \times 100\% \end{aligned}$$
  • MAE: interpretable, robust to outliers, same units as y
  • RMSE: penalizes large errors heavily; differentiable; outlier sensitive
  • R²: proportion of variance explained; use adjusted R² for multiple features
  • MAPE: percentage error; unstable when y ≈ 0
Classification Metrics
$$\begin{aligned} \text{Accuracy} &= (TP+TN)/(TP+TN+FP+FN) \\ \text{Precision} &= TP/(TP+FP) \quad \text{(quality of positives)} \\ \text{Recall} &= TP/(TP+FN) \quad \text{(coverage of positives)} \\ F_1 &= 2 \cdot P \cdot R / (P+R) \quad \text{(harmonic mean)} \\ F_\beta &= (1+\beta^2) \cdot P \cdot R \;/\; (\beta^2 \cdot P + R) \end{aligned}$$
  • Accuracy: misleading when classes imbalanced (99% neg → always predict neg = 99% accuracy)
  • Precision: cost of false alarms (spam filter)
  • Recall: cost of missing positives (cancer detection)
  • F1: balance of both; F2 weights recall more; F0.5 weights precision more

Common Mistakes & Misconceptions
  • Optimizing accuracy on imbalanced data — predicting the majority class always achieves (1 − minority rate) accuracy. A fraud model with 99.9% accuracy that catches 0 frauds is useless. Default to PR-AUC or F1 for imbalanced problems
  • Not pre-registering the metric before training — choosing the metric that makes your model look best after seeing results is p-hacking for ML. Agree the primary metric with stakeholders before any training begins
  • Confusing RMSE and MAE intuitions — RMSE penalises large errors more heavily (squared); MAE treats all errors equally. Use RMSE when large errors are especially costly (e.g., revenue); MAE when all errors are treated equally or the data has outliers
  • "My AUC-ROC is 0.95, model is great" — AUC-ROC can be very high with extreme class imbalance even if the minority class recall is poor. Always pair AUC-ROC with PR-AUC and a confusion matrix at your chosen operating threshold
  • Reporting a single F1 score without the threshold — F1 depends on the classification threshold. Report F1 at the business-chosen threshold, not the sklearn default of 0.5. Consider F-beta (β > 1 weights recall more heavily) for high-recall requirements
How It's Used at Top Companies
  • Airbnb (search ranking): NDCG as the primary offline ranking metric; complemented by online A/B test on booking conversion rate — offline metric must correlate with the online business metric to be trusted
  • Stripe (fraud): precision@fixed-recall as the primary metric — business sets a minimum recall (e.g., catch 90% of fraud), then maximise precision (minimise false positives that block good transactions)
  • Netflix (recommendations): moved from RMSE (star rating prediction) to ranking metrics (NDCG, recall@k) after discovering RMSE improvements didn't translate to better user engagement — lesson in metric alignment
  • Google (search quality): human rater-based quality metrics (NDCG over human relevance labels) as ground truth; model AUC-ROC used as a proxy during development, human eval as the final gate
🎯 Interview Gold

What to say: "Metric selection is a business decision before it's a technical one. I always ask: what is the cost of false positives vs false negatives? Credit risk: false negative (missed default) is catastrophic — maximise recall. Spam: false positive (blocking real email) destroys trust — maximise precision. For imbalanced problems, PR-AUC not AUC-ROC — PR-AUC is honest about minority class performance. I agree the primary metric with stakeholders before any training and never change it after seeing results."

Common mistake to avoid saying: "I'll use accuracy." — Unless you can demonstrate class balance and that all errors have equal cost, accuracy signals a lack of ML maturity. Almost always start with AUC-ROC or PR-AUC.

Expect the follow-up: "Why is AUC-ROC optimistic on imbalanced data?" — Answer: AUC-ROC includes True Negatives in the denominator (specificity = TN/(TN+FP)). When negatives dominate, the model can have poor recall while still scoring well on specificity, inflating AUC-ROC. PR-AUC uses only positives and predicted positives — it's blind to the large TN pool and gives a truthful picture of minority-class detection.
